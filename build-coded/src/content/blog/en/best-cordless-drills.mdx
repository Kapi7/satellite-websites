## Why a Cordless Drill Is the First Tool You Should Own

If you could only have one power tool, it should be a cordless drill/driver. It drives screws, drills holes, and with the right bits, it handles everything from assembling furniture to installing a deck. A good cordless drill is the backbone of every toolbox — amateur or professional.

But the market is crowded. Walk into any home improvement store and you will find dozens of options ranging from $40 to $400. Which one is actually worth your money? We compared seven popular models across the criteria that matter most to help you find the right drill for your needs and budget.

## How We Evaluated

Every drill was assessed across five core criteria:

- **Power (torque):** Measured in inch-pounds (in-lbs). More torque means the drill can drive larger fasteners into harder materials.
- **Battery platform:** Voltage, amp-hour ratings, and compatibility with other tools in the brand's ecosystem.
- **Weight and ergonomics:** How the drill feels in your hand during extended use.
- **Features:** Speed settings, chuck type, LED lights, brushless motor, and extras.
- **Value:** Performance relative to price. A $200 drill that performs like a $300 drill scores high here.

## The Battery Ecosystem Decision

Here is something most drill reviews do not emphasize enough: the drill you buy today locks you into a battery platform for years. That $120 Ryobi drill might lead to $2,000 worth of Ryobi tools over the next decade. Choose a platform, not just a drill.

### Platform Overview

- **DeWalt 20V MAX:** 200+ tools. Dominant in the contractor market. Widely available batteries.
- **Milwaukee M18:** 250+ tools. Strongest in electrical and plumbing trades. Premium pricing.
- **Ryobi ONE+ 18V:** 300+ tools. Most affordable ecosystem. Sold exclusively at Home Depot.
- **Makita 18V LXT:** 200+ tools. Strong in woodworking and landscaping. Excellent build quality across the line.
- **Bosch 18V:** 50+ tools. Smaller ecosystem but excellent quality.
- **Craftsman V20:** 70+ tools. Growing rapidly. Available at Lowe's.

If you already own tools in a battery platform, buying a drill from that same brand almost always makes the most sense — even if another brand's drill is marginally better.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is a brushless drill worth the extra money?

Yes, for most buyers. Brushless motors are 30-50 percent more efficient, run cooler, last roughly twice as long as brushed motors, and deliver more torque per battery charge. The premium over brushed is usually only $30-50. The only reason to buy brushed is if you rarely use the drill — for a few holes a year, brushed is fine.

### What size drill do I actually need — 12V, 18V, or 20V?

For most homeowners, an 18V or 20V MAX drill is the right balance of power and weight. 12V drills are lighter and cheaper but run out of torque driving 3-inch deck screws or drilling large holes. 20V FlexVolt and 60V MAX drills are overkill unless you drill concrete or mix drywall mud regularly. If you only drive cabinet screws and hang pictures, a 12V is genuinely enough.

### How long should a cordless drill battery last?

A quality 4.0 Ah battery delivers 400-800 screws per charge with an 18V drill. Expect 3-5 years of useful life from a lithium-ion battery before capacity drops noticeably. Store batteries at 40-60 percent charge in a cool place, never fully drain them, and don't leave them on the charger indefinitely.

### Is a hammer drill function worth having?

Only if you drill into concrete, brick, or masonry more than a few times a year. The hammer feature is useful but noisy and slightly heavier. For occasional masonry work, a regular drill with a quality masonry bit gets the job done. For real concrete work (installing ledgers, mounting TV walls on brick), you actually want a dedicated rotary hammer, not a combi drill.

### DeWalt vs Milwaukee vs Makita vs Ryobi — which brand is best?

For professionals who need maximum performance, Milwaukee M18 FUEL leads in raw power and build quality. For the best balance of price, ecosystem, and availability, DeWalt 20V MAX XR is the safe choice. For the most comfortable drill and best Japanese build quality, Makita LXT. For homeowners who want the cheapest credible ecosystem, Ryobi ONE+ is unbeatable. All four will outlast you with reasonable care.

### Can I use a drill as an impact driver?

In a pinch, but it's the wrong tool for heavy screw-driving. A drill uses rotational force only, so driving a 3-inch screw into pressure-treated lumber will either stall the motor or strip the screw head. An impact driver adds concussive blows that drive long screws effortlessly. If you build decks, frame walls, or assemble furniture regularly, the two-tool combo kit is worth every dollar.

### What's the difference between drill voltage and amp-hours?

Voltage (V) determines the drill's maximum torque and power. Amp-hours (Ah) measure battery capacity, which determines runtime. An 18V 2.0Ah battery is the same power as 18V 5.0Ah — it just runs out of juice 2.5x faster. For occasional use, 2.0-3.0 Ah is plenty; for all-day work, upgrade to 4.0-6.0 Ah.

### How do I know when it's time to replace my drill?

Replace it when the chuck won't hold bits tightly, the trigger becomes inconsistent, the battery won't hold a charge after replacement, or the gearbox develops a grinding noise. Most repairs (chuck, switch, motor brushes) cost more than a new budget drill. Premium tools (DeWalt, Milwaukee, Makita) are worth repairing; big-box store brands usually aren't.
