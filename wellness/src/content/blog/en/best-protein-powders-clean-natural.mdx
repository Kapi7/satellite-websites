## How to Choose

**Whey isolate** — highest protein per calorie, fastest absorption, best for post-workout. Choose if you tolerate dairy and want maximum efficiency.

**Whey concentrate** — slightly more fat and carbs, creamier taste, retains more immunoglobulins. Choose for general daily use and smoothies.

**Plant blend** — complete amino acids when multiple sources are combined. Choose if you're dairy-free or prefer plant-based nutrition.

**What to avoid on the label:** sucralose, acesulfame potassium, artificial colors (Red 40, Yellow 5), carrageenan, maltodextrin, and "proprietary blends" that hide ingredient doses.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What makes a protein powder "clean"?

A clean protein powder has minimal, recognizable ingredients: the protein source, possibly natural sweeteners (stevia, monk fruit), real flavoring (cocoa, vanilla extract), and sometimes sunflower lecithin for mixability. No artificial sweeteners, no artificial colors, no proprietary blends, no maltodextrin fillers, and third-party tested for heavy metals.

### Whey or plant protein — which is better?

For muscle-building and amino acid completeness, whey is superior due to its leucine content and digestibility. Plant blends (pea + rice + hemp) can match whey's profile when properly combined and are the right choice for dairy intolerance or a plant-based approach. Choose based on digestion and ethics, not marketing.

### How much protein do I actually need per day?

General health: 0.8 g per kg bodyweight. Active adults: 1.2-1.6 g/kg. Building muscle or losing weight while preserving muscle: 1.6-2.2 g/kg. For a 70 kg adult lifting weights, that's roughly 110-150 g daily — usually 25-40 g from a shake and the rest from whole food.

### Is protein powder necessary if I eat enough meat and eggs?

No. Protein powder is a convenience tool, not a requirement. If you comfortably hit your protein target from whole food, you don't need it. Many people use it because a shake is faster than cooking when they're short on time.

### Are whey isolates worth the extra cost over concentrates?

If you're lactose-sensitive, yes — isolates strip out nearly all lactose. If you tolerate dairy fine, concentrates are nutritionally similar and cheaper. Paying a premium for isolate only makes sense for dairy sensitivity or very low-carb goals.

### Do "grass-fed" whey labels matter?

Modestly. Grass-fed whey contains slightly higher omega-3s and CLA, but the differences are small in a protein powder context because you're getting concentrated protein, not fat. Prioritize third-party testing and ingredient simplicity over the grass-fed claim.

### Should I drink protein before, during, or after a workout?

Within about two hours of training is the practical window, and pre- or post-workout both work. Hitting your total daily protein target matters far more than timing. A post-workout shake is usually easiest logistically.

### Can I use protein powder if I'm trying to lose weight?

Yes — and it's one of the better tools for it. High protein intake preserves muscle during a calorie deficit, increases satiety, and raises the thermic effect of food. A shake that replaces a lower-protein snack is a practical weight-loss move.
